[1] Introduction

This document is a living summary of the pulsar survey program with the DSA-2000. The
intended audience is current and potential DSA-2000 team members, and those more
generally interested in working on the survey. Below I introduce basic specifications of the
telescope and the planned surveys relevant to the Chronoscope1, and detail plans for
implementation and operations of the latter. I also summarize the expected data products.
The pulsar survey is limited to the search for periodic (with potential acceleration due to
binary orbital motion) signals, and the FRB survey is limited to the search for isolated
pulses with no assumptions about time-correlation. Both surveys search for signals at
different dispersion measures (DMs).

[1.1] DSA-2000 reference

The Chronoscope surveys will be executed commensally with all DSA-2000 operations, and
will use compute hardware running in parallel with the Radio Camera, Pulsar Timing, and
other potential pipelines.

[1.2] Specifications of the Chronoscope/Pulsar survey

Two data-processing pipelines for pulsar searching will be implemented on the
Chronoscope hardware, enabling
- a targeted search in (nominally) 4000 beams steered towards continuum sources, and
- a blind search in (nominally) 1e6 beams that tile the central part of the DSA-2000
   primary beam.

The baseline plan for the targeted pulsar search is to steer full-array beams towards
continuum sources detected in early survey epochs. A few x 1e4 of sources are expected
per unique survey pointing, and we anticipate that via exclusion of resolved surveys, and
cross-matching with multi-wavelength surveys, we can reduce the number of candidate
pulsars per pointing to ~4000. This process will likely be less efficient in regions of the
Galactic Plane, where the numbers of pulsars per pointing wil exceed a few hundred. This is
where the blind search, with reduced sensitivity or time-sharing, can be applied.

Fiducial parameters relevant to the pulsar searches are given below.
 Parameter                      Targeted search               Blind search
 Number of beams                4000                          2e5
 Channels                       2500                          2500
 Band                           Approx. bottom 25%            Approx. bottom 25%
 DM trials                      500                           500
 Time resolution                0.1ms                         0.1ms
 Maximum dwell time             1260s                         60s
 Period range                   1ms – 5s                      1ms – 5s
 Acceleration trials            500                           5
 Polarizations                  I                             I
 Number of bits                 4                             4

We do not plan to store search-mode data. Instead, all searching needs to be done in
quasi-real time, on the timescale of the dwell time noted in the table above. The dwell time
is bounded strongly by the memory requirements, and less strongly by the compute
requirements. Typical pointings are anticipated to be around 21 min in duration.

The targeted-search beams will also be used for follow-up folding observations to confirm
and time pulsar discoveries. Follow-up occurs on two timecales. First, the 6x5-pointing
mosaics tiles will be observed over a ~week, with significant overlap between adjacent
pointings at the bottom of the band. This enables some beams to be steered towards
pulsars and pulsar candidates providing ~6 epochs over ~1 week. Second, the tiles will be
repeated, at least a few times, and ~10 times in 5500 deg^2 containing the Galactic Plane
and the Bulge.

The parameters given above will be extended if compute resources allow. Major extensions
include the number of channels, the number of beams in the blind search, and the dwell
time for the blind search. The dwell time refers to the longest-duration fully coherent
search that is envisaged, including all acceleration trials. Extensions in the dwell time, and
the number of channels and beams, provide S/N improvements that scale as the square
root of the extension factor.

[1.3] Specifications of the Chronoscope/FRB survey

The FRB search is commensal, conducted in real time in a large number of beams that tile
the FoV.

Fiducial parameters relevant to the FRB search are given below.
 Parameter                      Value
 Number of beams                6e6
 Channels                       2500
 Band                           Approx. bottom 25%
 DM trials                      2000
 Time resolution                1ms
 Width trials                   9 (2^0 to 2^8 samples)
 Maximum DM (pc/cc)             5000
 Polarizations                  I
 Number of bits                 4
 Max trigger latency            DM delay * 1.2

The search is expected to provide low-latency triggers upon candidate detection to enable
voltage dumps. The search occurs in two stages: naïve peak-finding, followed by a
clustering / correlation analysis of the peaks to identify likely candidates.

The parameters given above will be extended if compute resources allow. The number of
channels is the major potential extension, potentially providing S/N improvements that
scale as the square root of the number of channels. Additional width and DM trials will
provide more marginal improvements.

[2] Pipeline design considerations

[2.1] Overview
Block diagrams of the pipelines are shown below in Figures 1 and 2. The pipelines begin
with beamformer blocks, which in our terms also include dedispersion transforms. These
are the most compute intensive stages of the pipelines, outputting time-series data for
different beams / DMs, and potentially a few frequency sub-bands. In the case of the FRB
search, little additional processing is required besides peak finding and clustering. The
search is expected to be dynamic in the sense of the search thresholds being responsive to
the detection rate. In the case of the pulsar search, time-series data need to be
transformed to a periodicity basis (via, e.g., FFT, or a streaming folding algorithm) prior to
searching. An iterative approach is assumed, where probabilistic choices will be made to
reject datasets based on partial searches. In both pipelines, candidates trigger the
preparation of output metadata based on further analysis of specific datasets from the
buffered time series.

[2.2] Computational considerations for the beamformer, including dedispersion

For the targeted pulsar search, we envisage a voltage beamformer followed by
dedispersion. Given input voltage data from NA antennas in NC channels of width FC, the
compute required to form NB beams is approximately
RIN = 16NANCFC bits/s, assuming 4-bit complex data
OPS = 16NANCNBFC operations / s
ROUT = 4NBNC/Tsamp bits/s, assuming 4-bit intensities.

For the fiducial numbers in the tables above, we have RIN = 8.6 Tb/s, OPS = 34.4 POPS, and
ROUT = 0.4 Tb/s. The beamforming operation is dominated by matrix multiplications with
approximate dimensions M=NB, K=NA, and N can be tuned to be as large or as small as
required.

Brute-force dedispersion, given NDM DM trials and a sampling time of Tsamp, requires
approximately
OPS = 2NBNCNDM/ Tsamp operations / s
ROUT = 16NBNDM/ Tsamp bits/s, assuming 16-bit time series.

For the fiducial numbers in the tables above, we have OPS = 100 TOPS and R OUT = 0.32 Tb/s.
With a data preparation step that involves FFT-ing each time-series in each channel and
beam (a relatively small compute load), the dedispersion can be entirely done via matrix
operations, followed by a relatively small output step where inverse-FFTs are applied.

Forming a significantly larger number of beams via voltage beamforming is not feasible,
given the already high compute costs that scale as N B. Thus visibility-based beamforming is
required. The compute is approximately
OPS = 8(NA+1)NANCFC + 16NBlog2(NB)NC/Tsamp operations / s
with input and output data rates calculated as before. For fiducial numbers this is around
13 POPS, and ROUT = 60 Tb/s. The operations are dominated by matrix multiplications with
approximate dimensions M=NA, K>=256, and N=NA.

Compute costs for dedispersion are also similar feasible. However, the memory
requirements for dedispersion are significant. The DM delay is given by
TDM = 4.15 * DM * (F1,GHz-2 – F2,GHz-2) ms.
For a kernel to compute a single time sample at each DM, an amount of data totaling T DM in
time must be buffered at either the input or the output.2 Considering the FRB search, and
assuming 2500 channels above 0.8GHz are used, TDM = 16s. Given the output data rate of
the beamformer, which would not change significantly post dedispersion, this corresponds
to 120 TB. Some minor compromises could be made to make this feasible, for example,
shifting the lower frequency to 1 GHz, or lowering the number of beams. If this were done,

However, it is also possible to further integrate the beamforming and dedispersion steps to

multiplications is, for example, increased by NCDM. Post-beamforming, fine dedispersion is
done for each of the coarse DM trials.

[2.3] Computational considerations for pulsar searching

Real-time pulsar searching can be a major sink of computational resources, including
memory. Pulsar searching involves transforming input time-series into a basis that
separates signals of different periodicities. In practise, pulsar searching usually involves
the Fourier analysis of data spanning an entire observation. The total data volume for an
observation would be
DPSR = NBNDMTOBS/ Tsamp bytes, assuming 8-bit data
For the fiducial numbers in the tables above, including TOBS = 21min, DPSR = 25.2TB (targeted
search), and DPSR = 6.3PB (blind search). A factor of two greater memory is likely required
for processing.

Some savings can be realized. For example, the overlap-add method can be used to
combine DFTs of time-chunks. For example, given the period range of interest, all
frequency information is contained within 10s of data. The compute costs are
OPS = 5NACCNBNDM/ Tsamp * log2(TOBS/Tsamp)
RIO = 3NACCNBNDM/ Tsamp bytes/s, assuming out of place FFT
Considering TOBS = 10s, we have OPS = 850 TOPS and RIO = 30 TB/s (targeted search), and
OPS = 2.1 POPS and RIO = 75 TB/s (blind search).

Folding operations, with computational complexity O(NsampNcandidates), will be applied where
possible to maximize S/N. For example, once candidate periodicities are identified using a
part of the data, the remainder of the data can be folded on these periods. After an
observation, the data will anyway need to be folded on all candidate periodicities. We also
intend to investigate full Keplerian template searches, which involve convolution
operations.

[4] Implementation plans

A vast body of software exists that performs all functionality incorporated within the
Chronoscope. For example, tensor-core implementations exist for both the voltage-
beamforming3 and correlation4 steps. Various high-performance FFT libraries exist,
including cuFFT/cuFFTDx, vkFFT, and tcFFT. Dedispersion on GPUs is well-developed, e.g.,
in the dedisp5 and AstroAccelerate6 libraries, with well-understood performance.
Moderate-performance FRB and pulsar search codes exist.

However, it is unlikely that existing codes meet our performance requirements within a
reasonable hardware cap. We have thus embarked on a process to implement an
integrated pipeline within the framework of the quda7 package, which offers several
advantages in terms of e.g., memory-management, auto-tuning, and integration of kernels.
The development is focused at present on the beamforming and dedispersion steps, which
dominate the compute. In parallel, to enable the testing of assumptions regarding
performance of target hardware architectures, we have embarked on the creation of a
benchmarking package, described below in Section [7].

The target hardware for the Chronoscope is two Vera Rubin NVL144 racks (see Figure 3
below). Each rack includes 18 trays of compute, fully connected via NVLink. Each tray
contains two Vera CPUs, and two packages of four Rubin GPU dies each. Each GPU has
144GB of HBM4 memory, and each CPU has 1.5TB of likely LPDDR6X memory, addressible
on the GPU via NVLink-C2C. Interconnect between racks via CX9 is possible at up to
28.8TB/s, which is also the data ingest rate. Based on the existing Grace Blackwell NVL72
systems, each rack is likely to have 144 NVMe drives, which can be, e.g., 8TB each with 1-3
DWPD8, providing a PB of storage per rack.

[5] Data products

The Chronoscope surveys will produce data products on the cadence of candidate output.
These will be metered based on intelligence within the search systems (the Search
Controllers in Figures 1 and 2).

Each FRB candidate will trigger the storage of the following:
- FP16 intensity data for 64 nearby beams at the time of detection, assuming 4 frequency
   sub-bands, and 4s post-dedispersion: 2MB. After additional processing, this can be
   reduced by 64x to 32kB.
- 4-bit voltage data for 16 beams formed around each FRB, assuming full-band data, 4s
   post-dedispersion, full-pol: 334GB. After additional processing, this can be reduced by
   16x to 21GB. These data will only be stored for real FRBs, as determined by this
   processing.
- Pipeline metadata, including lowest-level candidate detections for a few s surrounding
   each event.
Each pulsar candidate will trigger the storage of the following:
- Dedispersed intensity data for the detection beam, assuming 4 frequency sub-bands,
   2160s, and 4-bit data: 43.2 MB
- Pipeline metadata, including summary of candidates detected during a pointing.

Each time a pulsar is timed with the Chronoscope, the following data will be stored.
- A Stokes I folded profile in 500 channels and 512 phase bins, assuming 4-byte data and
   10s subints: 110MB.

We anticipate metering the output of the Chronoscope as follows, resulting in the given
data volumes.
- 1 FRB candidate and single pulse from unknown pulsar per 4s on average, with long-
    term storage of intensity data only: 1.26TB total
- 1 real FRB per 600s, with long-term storage of intensity and voltage data: 5.52PB total
- Up to 100 pulsar candidates per pointing on average, for future follow-up: 332TB total,
    assuming 6x12801 pointings.9
- Up to 100 pulsars timed per pointing on average: 768TB total, assuming 6x12801
    pointings.

No significant processing of these data is anticipated within the DAT subsystem, besides
the minor editing of headers with additional observation metadata.

[7.1] Visibility-based beamforming and dedispersion

Given input complex voltage data of shape [Frequency, Time, Antenna], with N_F
frequencies, N_T times, and N_A antennas, the following operations are performed:
    1. Weight application. Apply frequency-dependent weights via Hadamard product to
       input complex voltage data. The weighs will have shape [Frequency, Antenna].
    2. Coarse dedispersion. For N_CD coarse DM trials out to DM_MAX, do a brute-force
       dedispersion operation to generate an array of shape [Coarse DMs, Coarse
       frequency, Coarse Time, Fine frequency, Fine time, Antenna]. The number of coarse
       frequencies is N_CF = N_F / N_CD, and the number of fine frequencies is N_CD. The
       number of fine times is, e.g., N_FT = 128. The number of coarse times is low: N_CT =
       N_T / N_CD / N_FT.
    3. Matrix multiplication. Next, each sub-array of [Fine frequency * Fine time, Antenna]
       is multiplied by its hermitian transpose with m=N_A, n=N_A, k=N_FT*N_CD, such
       that we end up with an array of shape [Coarse DMs, Coarse frequency, Coarse Time,
       Baseline]. The matrix multiplication can be implemented efficiently with, e.g., the
       CUTLASS HERK operation.
    4. Fine dedispersion. Here, we can apply a simple brute-force approach (e.g., see
       https://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.3847/1538-4365/acfef6#apjsacfef6s4) as a
       starting point to calculate the full range of DM trials. In each case, the accumulator
       must be a uniform UV grid, not the baseline array. This can be accomplished with a
       lookup table of grid indices of shape [Coarse frequency, Baseline]. The output array
       will then be [DMs, Coarse Time, Grid cell].
    5. Imaging. Finally, each grid will be subject to a 2D FFT to form beams, such that the
       output array will be [DMs, Coarse Time, Beams].

Each step can be benchmarked independently. In practise, some concurrency is likely
achievable, especially between operations that are memory-access intensive like
dedispersion, and more compute intensive, like matrix multiplication.

Reasonable numbers include:
N_F = 2048
N_T = 1e7
N_A = 1650
At 8-bit complex, this will use 33792GB of memory for the inputs to steps [1] and [2], which
is not reasonable for benchmarking. I suggest benchmarking step [1] as a straightforward
parallel Hadamard product on the CPU in a reasonably large (e.g., ~20GB) input array, with
similar ratios between N_F : N_T : N_A as the real data. You can assume FP16 input and
FP16 weights for this initial test.
Step [2] is best benchmarked on a per-antenna basis. I suggest using the CPU (via a multi-
threaded implementation) to operate on data of dimensions [N_F, N_T] for each antenna
sequentially to populate a GPU-memory array of shape [Coarse DMs, Coarse frequency,
Fine frequency, Fine time, Antenna] (dimensions [N_CD, N_CF, N_CD, N_FT, N_A]). This will
test memory access speeds, and the resulting timing can be scaled to multi-CPU systems.
Reasonable numbers:
N_CD = 16
N_CF = N_F / N_CD = 128
N_FT = 128
For the dedispersion, consider uniformly spaced DM trials in the range [0,5000) pc/cc, with
channel frequencies given in MHz by array(2048)*0.1302+F_LO, where a good starting
point for F_LO is 700MHz. Consider time sampling of 1/0.1302MHz = 7.68us. Given that this
operation simply involves moving data around, I suggest moving vectors with lengths that
assume 8-bit depths for the voltage data.

The matrix multiplication in step [3] can be done from/to GPU HBM, via a strided batched
CUTLASS HERK operating on FP8 data. Again, appropriate scaling can be applied to the
timing results. The data can be output in FP16.

Step [4] in principle operates on a very large array, and benchmarks must therefore be split
up. It’s likely best to consider a single coarse DM, and use something like 2e4 baselines.
Using N_CT = 5000 would be appropriate. The data can be assumed to be FP8 on the input,
accumulated to FP16 in the dedispersed grids.

Finally, step [5] represents a straightforward set of 2D FFTs. I suggest assuming FP16
complex data, and performing complex-to-real FFTs. Batching is likely preferred. I suggest
comparing cuFFT and tcFFT with identical plans.

The above can be run with different input parameters to benchmark visibility-based
beamforming and dedispersion for the full-field pulsar- and FRB-search cases. It would be
interesting to look at various scalings, e.g., with N_CD, and N_FT, as well as with N_F and
F_LO.

[7.2] Voltage beamforming

The dominant step here is the brute-force multiplications needed to perform the voltage-
based beamforming. These are only feasible if tensor cores are used. I suggest
benchmarking voltage-based beamforming assuming FP16 complex input data, using a
simple configuration passed to CUTLASS strided batched GEMMs, e.g., data of shape
[Frequency, Coarse time, Fine time, Antenna] where the sub-matrices [Fine time, Antenna]
of shape [16, 2048] (m=2048, k=16) are multiplied by weights matrices of shape [Weights,
Fine time] (n=4000, k=16). It’s likely best to assume that the weights matrix is also strided,
rather than doing something fancier. This is possible in the case where we need to steer the
beams as a function of time. I would try a reasonable N_F and N_CT such that the input
data size is a few tens of GB.

Then, I think it is worth giving the same problem to the TCBF wrapper around ccglib, and do
a comparative benchmark.

[7.3] Pulsar searching

At this initial stage, the most important benchmarks for pulsar searching are the FFTs. This
will give us an idea of the urgency of exploring more exotic techniques. We want to
benchmark the performance of 1D real to complex FFTs of lengths between 2^18 and 2^24
points. We want to consider batched FFTs with total data lengths of a few tens of GB
(essentially using much of the HBM on a given superchip). For example, for 2^20-point
FFTs, the number of FFTs in the batch would be ~1e4.

We should try three libraries at this stage: cuFFT, tcFFT, and nvpl. The first two can operate
on FP16 data, but nvpl (which operates on CPUs) requires FP32 data. We should determine
the performance vs FFT length for these large-batch cases, and compare to expectations
for the architecture.
